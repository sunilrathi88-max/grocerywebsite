# North Indian Besan Kadhi Recipe: The Coriander Finish That Changes Everything

Kadhi has two lives in India. In Gujarat, it is thin, sweet and tamarind-bright. In Punjab and North India, it is thick, tangy, pakora-studded, and finished with a red chilli tadka that sits on top in a pool of sizzling ghee.

What separates a good North Indian kadhi from a great one is not the pakoras. It is the **dhania powder finish** — added in the last two minutes of cooking, off direct heat, where it blooms in the residual steam and rounds off the curd's sourness into something cohesive and deeply satisfying.

This recipe covers the full method — the curd preparation, the besan ratio, the pakoras, and most importantly the dhania powder technique that professional cooks know and home cooks often skip.

## The Besan to Curd Ratio: Getting Kadhi Right Every Time

The most common kadhi failure is curdling — the yoghurt splits in the heat, producing grainy, separated liquid instead of a smooth, thick sauce. The fix is besan. The starch in chickpea flour stabilises the protein matrix in the heated dahi, preventing protein denaturation at high temperatures.

**The correct ratio**: 2 tablespoons besan per 500ml full-fat dahi (beaten smooth), thinned with 500ml water.

Whisk the besan into the dahi thoroughly before any heat is applied. Bring to a simmer slowly over medium-low heat, stirring constantly for the first 10 minutes until the besan cooks through and the kadhi thickens to a coating consistency. Then simmer gently for 20 more minutes without stirring — this develops the characteristic slight tang and smooth texture.

> [!IMPORTANT]
> **The Secret to Smooth Kadhi**
> Getting the perfect, velvety texture in a kadhi is all about slow heating and the right ratio of besan to dahi. The besan acts as a stabilizer, preventing the yogurt from curdling when it hits the heat. By whisking them together before cooking and stirring constantly as you bring it to a simmer, you create a smooth, thick base that holds all the flavors together.

## The Dhania Powder Finish: Technique and Timing

Most kadhi recipes add dhania powder at the beginning with the other dry spices. This is incorrect. Coriander's primary aromatic compounds — linalool (60–70% of seed essential oil) and geraniol — are volatile. Extended cooking for 30–40 minutes at simmering temperatures destroys most of them, leaving only the base earthy notes.

**The correct technique**: In the last two minutes before serving, remove the kadhi from direct heat. Add three-quarters of a teaspoon of [Rathi Naturals Dhaniya Powder](/products/coriander-powder) directly to the simmering kadhi. Stir gently. The residual heat blooms the powder's aromatic compounds without destroying them.

> [!NOTE]
> **Timing the Aroma**
> Coriander's brightest aromatic notes are delicate and heat-sensitive. Adding the powder right at the end—after the main cooking is done—allows it to "bloom" in the residual heat. This preserves the lightest, citrusy scents that would otherwise be lost during a long simmer, giving your Kadhi a fresh, professional finish.

## The Crispy Pakora Recipe for Kadhi

**Ingredients (makes 16 pakoras)**:

- 100g besan
- half a teaspoon haldi powder
- half a teaspoon ajwain
- one teaspoon [Rathi Naturals Dhaniya Powder](/products/coriander-powder)
- salt & half a teaspoon red chilli powder
- 80–90ml water (add gradually)
- oil for frying

**Method**:

1. Mix besan, spices and salt. Add water slowly until you have a thick, dropping consistency.
2. Heat oil to 170°C. Drop spoonfuls of batter. Fry 4 minutes per side until deep golden.
3. Add to kadhi in the last 10 minutes of cooking — pakoras absorb the kadhi beautifully.

> [!TIP]
> **Pakora Texture**
> For the best "soaking" pakoras, ensure your batter is thick enough to hold its shape. Adding a pinch of dhaniya powder to the batter not only adds flavor but also helps the pakora retain its structure once it's dropped into the hot kadhi.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Why does my kadhi curdle?**
A: Curdling happens when the besan ratio is too low or the heat is too high initially. Use 2 tablespoons of besan per 500ml dahi minimum, and heat slowly while stirring constantly.

**Q: Can I make kadhi without pakoras?**
A: Yes — plain kadhi without pakoras (kadhi sada) is common. Some people add boiled potato cubes or drumstick instead.

**Q: When should I add dhania powder to kadhi?**
A: In the last 2 minutes, off direct heat. This preserves the lightest aromatic notes that make the dish truly fragrant.
