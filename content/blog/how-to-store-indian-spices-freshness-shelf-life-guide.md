## Recipe Deep-Dive: Technique, Ratios and Common Mistakes

The specific ratios and technique for this recipe have been developed through decades of Indian cooking tradition. The standard approach — adding pure haldi powder at the wrong phase of cooking — is the most common error that prevents home cooks from achieving professional results. The correct method positions pure haldi powder at the phase in the recipe where its aromatic compounds can bloom without being destroyed: in hot fat at the beginning for foundational flavour, or in the last minutes of cooking for aromatic finishing. Never add cold-ground spices powder to boiling water directly without a fat phase intermediary. The fat extracts the oil-soluble aromatic compounds first, then they distribute evenly through the entire dish as water is added. This principle applies across Indian cooking: every spice works better when bloomed in oil or ghee first.

> [!TIP]
> **The Glass Jar Rule**
> Plastic containers are slightly porous and can absorb the essential oils of your spices over time. For the longest shelf life, transfer your Rathi spices into airtight glass containers. Dark or amber glass is even better, as it filters out the UV rays that fade the natural colors of turmeric and chili.
