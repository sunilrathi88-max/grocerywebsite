# Is Your Dhaniya Powder Pure? How to Test Coriander at Home

Coriander powder adulteration — primarily with starch, sand or **exhausted coriander** (already steam-distilled) — is among the most common spice adulterations in India. Because coriander is a beige/brown powder, it is incredibly easy for unscrupulous manufacturers to mix in fillers like ground husk or artificial starches.

Here are four simple home tests to reveal contamination in minutes.

## 1. The Water Float Test (Best for Husk/Sand)

Pure [Ramganj Mandi Coriander Powder](/products/coriander-powder) is made from oil-rich seeds.

- **Method**: Add a teaspoon of coriander powder to a glass of water. Do not stir.
- **The Result**:
  - **PURE**: Most of the powder will float for several minutes because of the natural oil content. It will eventually settle, but slowly.
  - **ADULTERATED**: Adulterants like sand, grit, or heavy starches will sink to the bottom immediately. Sawdust or light husk will float indefinitely and look 'fibrous' rather than like a powder.

## 2. The Iodine Test (Best for Starch)

If you have a medical-grade iodine solution (like Betadine), you can check for potato starch or cereal fillers.

- **Method**: Mix a small amount of dhaniya powder with water to make a thin liquid. Add 2-3 drops of iodine.
- **The Result**:
  - **PURE**: The color stays brownish/yellow.
  - **ADULTERATED**: If it turns deep blue or black, starch is present.

## 3. The Aroma Test (Best for 'Exhausted' Spices)

The most common 'legal' adulteration is using seeds that have already had their oils extracted for perfume.

- **Method**: Rub a pinch of powder between your palms until they feel warm.
- **The Result**:
  - **PURE**: You should get a hit of citrus, pine, and floral notes (Linalool).
  - **ADULTERATED**: It will smell like 'nothing' or like plain dry grass/soil.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Q: Does dark coriander powder mean it is more pure?**
A: Not necessarily. In fact, very dark powder often indicates it was over-heated during grinding or that a coloring agent was added to hide the presence of starch. Quality, cold-ground dhaniya is a muted, vibrant greenish-beige.

**Q: How long does pure dhaniya powder last?**
A: In an airtight container, 18-24 months. If it loses its smell, the oils have volatilized, and it's time to replace it.
